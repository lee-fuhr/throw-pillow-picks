Throw pillow questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Throw pillows are an easy way to refresh a room, but the right choice depends on fill, cover material, size, and how you plan to use them. This guide answers the most common questions so you can buy with confidence and avoid costly mistakes.

What size throw pillow should I buy?

The most versatile size for a standard sofa is 18 to 20 inches square. For a loveseat or armchair, 16 to 18 inches usually looks balanced. On a king or queen bed, a row of 20-inch or 22-inch pillows works well, often paired with a lumbar or bolster. If your furniture is oversized or very modern, you can go up to 24 inches. The key is to measure your seat depth and back height so the pillow doesn’t overpower the piece or get lost. A pillow that sticks out past the armrest by an inch or two is generally fine.

What is the best fill for throw pillows?

Down and down-alternative fills are the most popular for comfort and that soft, sink-in feel. Down clusters are pricier but hold their shape well with occasional fluffing. Down-alternative is cheaper, hypoallergenic, and easier to clean, though it may flatten a bit faster. Polyester fiberfill is the most budget-friendly and very resilient, but it can feel stiff or lumpy over time. Foam core or shredded foam inserts give a firmer, more structured look that holds sharp corners, good for decorative pillows that won’t be slept on. Cotton or kapok fills are natural options but tend to be less durable.

How many throw pillows should I use on a couch?

For a typical three-seater sofa, three to five pillows creates a balanced look. An odd number often feels more natural and allows for variety in size and shape. On a loveseat, two to three pillows is usually enough. The goal is to keep the sofa functional, so leave at least one seating spot free of pillows. If you have a sectional, you can add one or two pillows per seat section, but avoid overcrowding. A good rule: start with one large square at each end, add a medium square or lumbar in the middle, then finish with a small accent in front.

How do I clean throw pillows?

Always check the care tag first. Removable covers that are machine-washable are the easiest to maintain, wash them in cold water on a gentle cycle and air-dry to avoid shrinkage. Spot-clean non-removable or delicate covers with a mild detergent and a damp cloth. For down pillows, occasional fluffing and sunning helps freshen them. Polyester fills can go in the dryer on low heat with a couple of tennis balls to re-fluff. Avoid dry-cleaning unless the tag says it’s required, as chemicals can damage the fill or fabric over time.

Are expensive throw pillows worth it?

It depends on how you’ll use them. Higher-priced pillows often use better materials: hand-loomed fabrics, higher thread-count covers, and premium fills that stay plump longer. If your pillows will get daily use, leaning against, napping on, spending more for durable construction and a removable, washable cover can pay off. Budget-friendly options can look great in lower-traffic settings like a guest bedroom or a reading chair that’s mostly decorative. The biggest difference is often in the zipper quality, seam finishing, and fill density. Mid-range pillows from well-known home brands usually give the best value for most households.

Should I buy pillows with or without inserts?

Pillows sold with inserts are convenient and often come pre-fluffed, but the fill quality can vary. Many decorative pillows from big-box stores use a lower-grade fiberfill that flattens quickly. Buying covers and inserts separately gives you more control over fill type and firmness. You can upgrade to a down or shredded-foam insert inside a cover you love, and replace the insert later without tossing the cover. It also makes cleaning easier. The trade-off is that it costs a little more upfront and requires a bit of research to match sizes accurately.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use throw pillows on the floor?

Yes, but choose pillows with a durable, stain-resistant cover such as cotton canvas, polyester, or outdoor-grade fabric. Floor pillows need a thicker, firmer fill to hold up under sitting and lounging, so look for a high-density foam or kapok core.

Should throw pillows match my curtains?

They don’t need to match exactly, but a coordinated color palette or complementary texture helps tie the room together. For example, if your curtains are a solid neutral, pull an accent color from the curtain fabric into one or two pillows.

What is the ideal throw pillow firmness?

For decorative pillows that stay in place, a medium-firm to firm fill (like a high-quality down substitute or foam core) holds its shape best. For pillows you actually lean against, a medium-soft down or down-alternative insert is more comfortable.

How often should I replace throw pillows?

Pillows that are used daily and fluffed regularly usually last two to four years before the fill loses its loft. If a pillow no longer bounces back after compressing or looks lumpy, it’s time for a new insert, the cover may still be fine.

Do throw pillows need inserts?

Most ready-made throw pillows come with inserts, but if you buy separate covers, you will need an insert. Always buy an insert that is one to two inches larger than the cover to ensure a full, plump appearance (for example, a 20-inch insert for an 18-inch cover).

How do I keep throw pillows from sliding off a leather sofa?

Choose pillows with a textured or grippy backing fabric, or use non-slip rug pads cut to size and tucked under the pillow. Another trick is to buy pillows with a slightly larger fill so they wedge more snugly between the cushions.

In shortChoosing throw pillows doesn’t have to be overwhelming. Start with the right size for your furniture, pick a fill that matches your comfort needs and cleaning habits, and don’t be afraid to mix shapes and colors. A careful read of a pillow’s construction and care label will save you from sagging, fading, or a frustrating washing experience down the road.
